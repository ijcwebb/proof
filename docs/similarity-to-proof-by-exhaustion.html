An Introduction to Mathematical Proof

Chapter 14 Similarity to proof by exhaustion}

Recall that in a proof by exhaustion, we:

  • identify all the possibilities,
  • make sure that we haven’t forgotten any possibilities, and
  • prove \(P \rightarrow Q\) for each possibility.

In proof by cases, we use the same technique. The only difference is the list of possibilities are renamed “” and each single case is much broader (more general and less specific) than each single possibility in a proof by exhaustion.

For example, while the list of possibilities in a proof by exhaustion might be each whole number from 1 to 20 or each possible route taken by a knight on a chessboard, the cases in a proof by cases might be negative numbers and non-negative numbers, or even integers between 1 and 20 and odd integers between 1 and 20, or whole numbers and non-whole numbers.

Let’s compare a couple of conjectures we previously proved by exhaustion and a couple which can be proved using cases, to see the difference between in a proof by exhaustion and in a proof by cases.

\(a\) and \(b\) both \(< 0\)

If we want to demonstrate this conjecture to someone by asking them to pick two numbers, and they pick one negative number and one non-negative number, we can assign the negative one to \(b\) and the non-negative one to \(a\).\

Consider the conjecture

\[\begin{align*} \intertext{We are considering the product of two integers, $a \cdot b$. One of these integers is even, and the other is odd. \\ W.l.o.g., let's say that $a$ is the even one and $b$ is the odd one, so} a &= 2m \\ b &= 2n + 1 \intertext{with $m, n \in \mathbb Z$. Notice I have used two different integers here, $m$ and $n$, as the conjecture doesn't say $a$ and $b$ must be consecutive (though of course they can be).\\ We have} a \cdot b &= (2m)\cdot (2n+1) \\ &= 4mn + 2m\\ &= 2(2mn+m) \intertext{which is clearly even. \hspace{110mm}$\square$} \end{align*}\]

Again, if I wanted to demonstrate this conjecture to someone by asking them to pick two numbers, one even and one odd, I just assign the even one to \(a\) and the odd one to \(b\).

If we had made \(a\) the odd one and \(b\) the even one, the proof would look identical, so our choice of which was odd and which was even was unimportant. That’s what w.l.o.g. means!

Sometimes, however, w.l.o.g. doesn’t work. That’s because changing the letters fundamentally change the case we’re considering. Let’s look at an example…

Consider the conjecture

We are considering the product of two integers, \(a \cdot b\). If two integers \(a\) and \(b\) are consecutive, then \(b = a + 1\). If we multiply them, we get \[\begin{align*} a \cdot b &= (a)(a+1) \\ &= a^2 + a \intertext{but it isn't obvious that this is even, so we'll need a new strategy. \\ As $a$ and $b$ are consecutive integers, one is even, and the other is odd, because parity alternates for integers.\\ Let's make $a$ the even one and $b$ the odd one, so,} a &= 2m \\ b &= 2m + 1 \intertext{Their product is} (2m)\cdot (2m+1) &= 4m^2 + 2m\\ &= 2(2m^2+m) \intertext{which is clearly even. \\ But notice, this proof only covers pairs of consecutive integers where the smaller number is even, for example 2 and 3, 4 and 5, or $-2$ and $-1$. It does \textit{not} cover pairs of consecutive integers where the smaller number is odd. If you ask a friend to pick two consecutive numbers and they pick 7 and 8, we must assign 7 to $a$ as it's the smaller of the two numbers, but we \textit{can't} assign 7 to $a$ because we said "let's make $a$ the even one". \\ We can label the case above as \textbf{Case 1}, then provide a second case, where $a$ is odd and $b$ is even:\\ \textbf{Case 2: }} a &= 2m + 1 \\ b &= (2m + 1) + 1 = 2m + 2 \intertext{The product is} (2m + 1)\cdot (2m+2) &= 4m^2 + 4m + 2\\ &= 2(2m^2+2m + 1) \intertext{which is clearly even. \\ We've shown that this conjecture is \textsc{true} if the smaller of the two consecutive numbers is even, and if the smaller of the two consecutive numbers is odd. These two cases cover all possible pairs of consecutive numbers, and in both cases, we found the statement to be \text{true}, so the conjecture is \textsc{true}. We are done! \hspace{120mm}} \end{align*}\]

Here’s what the proof looks like in full: \[\begin{align*} \intertext{\textbf{Conjecture: }The product of two consecutive numbers is even. \\ \textbf{Proof:} We call our two consecutive numbers $a$ and $b$. W.l.o.g., let $a$ be the smaller of the two. \\ \textbf{Case 1:} $a$ even, $b$ odd.} a &= 2m \\ b &= 2m + 1 \intertext{Thus,} a \cdot b &= (2m)\cdot (2m+1) \\ &= 4m^2 + 2m\\ &= 2(2m^2+m) \intertext{which is clearly even. \\ \textbf{Case 2:} $a$ odd, $b$ even.} a &= 2m + 1 \\ b &= (2m + 1) + 1 = 2m + 2 \intertext{Thus,} a \cdot b &= (2m + 1)\cdot (2m+2) \\ &= 4m^2 + 4m + 2\\ &= 2(2m^2+2m + 1) \intertext{which is clearly even. \hspace{110mm}$\square$} \end{align*}\]
