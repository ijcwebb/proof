An Introduction to Mathematical Proof

Chapter 13 Proof by cases

13.1 Introduction

This week we have a beautiful method, called proof by cases. Proving something by cases is very similar to proving something by exhaustion.

13.2 Similarity to proof by exhaustion

Recall that in a proof by exhaustion, we:

  • identify all the possibilities,
  • make sure that we haven’t forgotten any possibilities, and
  • prove \(P \rightarrow Q\) for each possibility.

In proof by cases, we use the same technique. The only difference is the list of possibilities are renamed “cases,” and each single case is much broader (more general and less specific) than each single possibility in a proof by exhaustion.

For example, while the list of possibilities in a proof by exhaustion might be each whole number from 1 to 20 or each possible route taken by a knight on a chessboard, the cases in a proof by cases might be all negative numbers and all non-negative numbers, or all even integers between 1 and 20 and all odd integers between 1 and 20, or all whole numbers and all non-whole numbers.

Let’s compare a couple of conjectures we previously proved by exhaustion and a couple which can be proved using cases, to see the difference between possibilities in a proof by exhaustion and cases in a proof by cases.

13.3 Without loss of generality

In the table on the previous page, we referred to the phrase “without loss of generality,” or simply w.l.o.g. We can use it whenever simply switching the letters doesn’t fundamentally change the case we’re looking at.

Example 13.1 Consider conjecture 13.2 from the table:

Conjecture 13.2 : For \(a, b \in \mathbb R\), \(|a + b| \leq |a| + |b|\).

We use Case 1 to check if the conjecture is true when both \(a\) and \(b\) are non-negative. We use Case 2 to check if the conjecture is true when both \(a\) and \(b\) are negative. Finally, we should check if the conjecture is true if one of \(a\) and \(b\) is negative and the other is non-negative. We say, without loss of generality, that the negative one will be \(b\) and the non-negative one will be \(a\).

This means we need just three cases, not four:

Case 1: \(a\) and \(b\) both \(\geq 0\).

Case 2: \(a\) and \(b\) both \(< 0\).

Case 3: \(a \geq 0\) and \(b <0\).

If we want to demonstrate this conjecture to someone by asking them to pick two numbers, and they pick one negative number and one non-negative number, we can assign the negative one to \(b\) and the non-negative one to \(a\).

Example 13.2 Consider the conjecture

Conjecture 13.4 : The product of an odd integer and an even integer is even.

We are considering the product of two integers, \(a \cdot b\). One of these integers is even, and the other is odd.

W.l.o.g., let’s say that \(a\) is the even one and \(b\) is the odd one, so

\[\begin{align} a &= 2m \\ b &= 2n + 1 \end{align}\]

with \(m, n \in \mathbb Z\). Notice I have used two different integers here, \(m\) and \(n\), as the conjecture doesn’t say \(a\) and \(b\) must be consecutive (though of course they can be).

We have

\[\begin{align} a \cdot b &= (2m)\cdot (2n+1) \\ &= 4mn + 2m\\ &= 2(2mn+m) \end{align}\]

which is clearly even.

Again, if I wanted to demonstrate this conjecture to someone by asking them to pick two numbers, one even and one odd, I just assign the even one to \(a\) and the odd one to \(b\).

If we had made \(a\) the odd one and \(b\) the even one, the proof would look identical, so our choice of which was odd and which was even was unimportant. That’s what w.l.o.g. means!

Sometimes, however, w.l.o.g. doesn’t work. That’s because changing the letters does fundamentally change the case we’re considering. Let’s look at an example…

Example 13.3 Consider the conjecture

Conjecture 13.5 : The product of two consecutive numbers is even.34

We are considering the product of two integers, \(a \cdot b\). If two integers \(a\) and \(b\) are consecutive, then \(b = a + 1\). If we multiply them, we get

\[\begin{align} a \cdot b &= (a)(a+1) \\ &= a^2 + a \end{align}\]

but it isn’t obvious that this is even, so we’ll need a new strategy.

As \(a\) and \(b\) are consecutive integers, one is even, and the other is odd, because parity alternates for integers.

Let’s make \(a\) the even one and \(b\) the odd one, so,

\[\begin{align} a &= 2m \\ b &= 2m + 1 \end{align}\]

Their product is

\[\begin{align} (2m)\cdot (2m+1) &= 4m^2 + 2m\\ &= 2(2m^2+m) \end{align}\] which is clearly even.

But notice, this proof only covers pairs of consecutive integers where the smaller number is even, for example 2 and 3, 4 and 5, or \(-2\) and \(-1\). It does not cover pairs of consecutive integers where the smaller number is odd. If you ask a friend to pick two consecutive numbers and they pick 7 and 8, we must assign 7 to \(a\) as it’s the smaller of the two numbers, but we can’t assign 7 to \(a\) because we said “let’s make \(a\) the even one.”

We can label the case above as Case 1, then provide a second case, where \(a\) is odd and \(b\) is even:

Case 2:

\[\begin{align} a &= 2m + 1 \\ b &= (2m + 1) + 1 = 2m + 2 \end{align}\]

The product is

\[\begin{align} (2m + 1)\cdot (2m+2) &= 4m^2 + 4m + 2\\ &= 2(2m^2+2m + 1) \end{align}\]

which is clearly even.

We’ve shown that this conjecture is true if the smaller of the two consecutive numbers is even, and if the smaller of the two consecutive numbers is odd. These two cases cover all possible pairs of consecutive numbers, and in both cases, we found the statement to be true, so the conjecture is true. We are done!

Here’s what the proof looks like in full:

Proof. We call our two consecutive numbers \(a\) and \(b\). W.l.o.g., let \(a\) be the smaller of the two.

Case 1: \(a\) even, \(b\) odd.

\[\begin{align} a &= 2m \\ b &= 2m + 1 \end{align}\]

Thus,

\[\begin{align} a \cdot b &= (2m)\cdot (2m+1) \\ &= 4m^2 + 2m\\ &= 2(2m^2+m) \end{align}\]

which is clearly even.

Case 2: \(a\) odd, \(b\) even.

\[\begin{align} a &= 2m + 1 \\ b &= (2m + 1) + 1 = 2m + 2 \end{align}\]

Thus,

\[\begin{align} a \cdot b &= (2m + 1)\cdot (2m+2) \\ &= 4m^2 + 4m + 2\\ &= 2(2m^2+2m + 1) \end{align}\]

which is clearly even.

13.5 Choosing the cases

Once you have recognised that your proof will require cases, it can sometimes be difficult deciding how to split the proof up.

The most important thing to remember when using cases is to make them distinct (they should not overlap) and complete (they should consider all possible situations).

For example, if \(P\) says \(a\) is an integer and you split the proof into positive integers and negative integers, then you have forgotten to consider \(a = 0\), and your cases are not complete. You will have to amend one of the two cases to include \(a = 0\), or add it as its own case. Similarly, if you have the cases \(a \geq 0\) and \(a \leq 0\), then 0 is contained in both cases, meaning the cases overlap, which also isn’t good. You should amend one of the cases so it no longer includes 0.

Here are some examples of how you might split up a proof into cases, depending on what type of number the conjecture concerns:

\(a \in \mathbb Z\)

Case 1: \(a\) is even
Case 2: \(a\) is odd

Case 1: \(a = 3k\)
Case 2: \(a = 3k + 1\)
Case 3: \(a = 3k + 2\) with \(k \in \mathbb Z\)

\(a \in \mathbb R\)

Case 1: \(a\) is rational
Case 2: \(a\) is irrational

Case 1: \(|a| > 1\)
Case 2: \(|a| \leq 1\)

\(a \in \mathbb Z\) or \(\mathbb R\)

Case 1: \(a\) is positive
Case 2: \(a\) is negative
Case 3: \(a\) = 0

Pairs of numbers \(a, b\)

Case 1: both \(a, b > 0\)
Case 2: both \(a, b < 0\)
Case 3: \(a > 0\) and \(b < 0\) (w.l.o.g.)
Case 4: at least one of \(a\) and \(b\) equals 0

Case 1: \(a,b \neq 0\)
Case 2: at least one of \(a\) and \(b\) equals 0

Exercise 13.1 Verify that for the above examples, the cases are distinct and complete.

Note: You might split the proof into cases differently to a classmate. Don’t worry! There’s many ways to prove the same thing - just make sure that when splitting up the problem into cases, you don’t miss any numbers!

Once you have split the problem into parts, consider each part separately from one another.

13.6 How many cases?

For conjectures in this course that require a proof by cases, you will probably need 2, 3, or maybe 4 cases. However, there is no upper limit for how many you can use. Proofs involving between 5 and 10 cases are common. You should always try to use the smallest number of cases possible.

The first proof of the Four Colour Theorem35 used 1936 cases. Since then, mathematicians have managed to reduce this number to close to 600, which is still a lot, but less than 1936!
